About

Advancing Nursing Science
to Shape Health Policy

Matthew D. McHugh is Professor and Independence Chair for Nursing Education at the University of Pennsylvania School of Nursing, where he directs the Center for Health Outcomes and Policy Research (CHOPR). He is an elected member of the National Academy of Medicine, a Fellow of the American Academy of Nursing, and Senior Fellow at the Leonard Davis Institute of Health Economics.

The fundamental question underlying Dr. McHugh’s program of research is “How does nursing influence the achievement of our most important national and international health policy goals?” He directs highly visible studies that draw on his expertise in nursing, law, public health, and health services research to evaluate how nursing can be a force for quality, equity, and innovation in health services. His work has directly informed policy discussions at state, national, and international levels, including safe nurse staffing policy in California, Pennsylvania, New York, and Australia.

McHugh holds a PhD in Nursing from Penn, an MPH from Harvard, and a JD from Northeastern University School of Law—bringing disciplinary depth to the intersection of health policy, law, and nursing science. He began his career as a registered nurse and family nurse practitioner, grounding his research in clinical practice.

Center for Health Outcomes and Policy Research

Research Focus

CHOPR is a multidisciplinary research center at Penn Nursing dedicated to understanding how features of nursing—staffing, work environments, and education—shape patient outcomes and health policy. Visit CHOPR →    CHOPR History →

Nurse Staffing & Policy

Evaluating the effects of nurse-to-patient ratio legislation on staffing levels, patient mortality, readmissions, and cost—with studies in California, Pennsylvania, New York, and Queensland, Australia.

Explore research →

Corporatization in Healthcare

Examining how hospital ownership, consolidation, and market pressures shape nursing practice environments, workforce composition, and patient outcomes across health systems.

Explore research →

Clinician Burnout

Documenting the prevalence, predictors, and consequences of burnout among nurses and clinicians—and identifying organizational and policy interventions that address its root causes.

Explore research →

Work Environments

Linking organizational conditions—leadership, staffing, governance, and Magnet Recognition—to nurse retention, patient safety, and care quality across thousands of healthcare organizations

Explore research →

Health Equity & Disparities

Investigating how nursing resources mediate racial and ethnic disparities in surgical outcomes, cardiac arrest survival, readmissions, and care for underserved populations.

Explore research →

Policy Evaluation & Methods

Applying quasi-experimental methods, matched cohort designs, and causal inference to evaluate large-scale health policy interventions including the ACA Graduate Nurse Education Demonstration.
