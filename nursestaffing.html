Nurse Staffing & Policy

Research Overview

Nurse staffing — the number of patients assigned to each nurse — is one of the most consequential and extensively studied factors in hospital care quality. McHugh's research has helped establish the causal link between staffing levels and patient outcomes including mortality, readmissions, failure to rescue, and length of stay, and has shaped major policy debates in the United States and internationally.

A landmark contribution is the prospective evaluation of Queensland, Australia's minimum nurse-to-patient ratio mandate, published in The Lancet (2021), which demonstrated that the policy reduced mortality, readmissions, and length of stay — while generating cost savings that exceeded the cost of additional staffing. Related work has examined California's ratio law, showing it reduced nurse burnout and improved patient outcomes without reducing nursing workforce quality. Studies in New York and other states have estimated the public health impact of proposed staffing legislation.

This body of work addresses not just whether staffing matters, but how policy can be designed and evaluated to improve it at scale — with implications for hospital administrators, state legislatures, and federal policymakers. Check out our Safe Staffing White Paper.
