Corporatization in Healthcare

Research Overview

The transformation of American hospitals through consolidation, private equity acquisition, and the growth of for-profit ownership has profound implications for nursing practice and patient care. McHugh's research examines how hospital ownership structures and market pressures shape the conditions under which nurses work — and the outcomes patients experience.

Studies have compared nurse work environments, staffing levels, and patient outcomes across not-for-profit, for-profit, and government-owned hospitals, consistently finding that ownership type is associated with meaningful differences in organizational investment in nursing. For-profit hospitals have been shown to have worse nurse work environments and staffing, which in turn is associated with higher rates of nurse burnout and worse patient outcomes including mortality, readmissions, and patient satisfaction.

More recent work has examined the growing role of private equity in hospital ownership, tracking how acquisition changes staffing ratios, nursing leadership, and clinical outcomes over time. This research raises fundamental questions about whether market-driven models of hospital governance are compatible with the organizational conditions necessary to deliver high-quality nursing care — and what role policymakers can play in setting minimum standards that protect patients regardless of ownership structure.
