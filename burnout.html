Nurse Burnout & Well-Being

Research Overview

Nurse burnout is a crisis with direct consequences for patient care, workforce retention, and the sustainability of hospital systems. McHugh's research has documented the prevalence, predictors, and outcomes of burnout among nurses across the United States and internationally, identifying both the human and institutional costs of poor working conditions. Key studies have shown that burnout and job dissatisfaction among hospital nurses are strongly associated with worse patient satisfaction, lower care quality ratings, and higher intent to leave — creating a cycle of understaffing that compounds the problem. Research using data from tens of thousands of nurses across multiple states has tracked how burnout has evolved before, during, and after the COVID-19 pandemic, finding that nurse dissatisfaction and intent to leave have reached their highest recorded levels in the post-pandemic period. This work emphasizes that burnout is not primarily a personal resilience problem but an organizational one — rooted in poor staffing, unsupportive work environments, and inadequate management responsiveness — and that structural interventions are more effective than individual wellness programs.
